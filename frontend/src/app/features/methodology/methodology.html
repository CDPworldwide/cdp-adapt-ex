Methodology

This platform draws from both CDP's annual questionnaire for reporting by governments and supplemental hazard data to cover the climate hazards that cities, states, and regions face and how they are addressing them. It also surfaces insights from around the globe by identifying relevant solutions and actions across cities with similar characteristics.

CDP's annual questionnaire

Cities, states, and regions are invited to complete CDP's annual questionnaire through the CDP-ICLEI Track disclosure portal. The questionnaire captures qualitative and quantitative information on topics including governance, climate risk and vulnerability assessments, greenhouse gas emissions inventories, energy consumption, sectoral data, adaptation and mitigation goals, and climate planning and actions. Jurisdictions can update their disclosures through questionnaires released yearly. The Explorer currently displays data from 2025 public disclosures.

Hazards

For jurisdictions that have disclosed information through the questionnaire, hazard data is primarily drawn from this disclosure. Additional hazard information and data for visualization is sourced from remote sensing and model-derived data accessed through Google Earth Engine.

Cities, states, and regions questionnaire data

Cities, states, and regions are asked to share details on the most significant climate hazards facing their jurisdiction (Question 2.2 in the 2025 questionnaire). The self-disclosed hazard data may contain information about vulnerable population and sectoral exposure, hazard probability, magnitude of impact, expected hazard changes in intensity or frequency, and the timeframe of expected change. Probability and magnitude responses are given along a five-category scale: low, medium-low, medium, medium-high, and high.

For each jurisdiction, up to four top hazards are displayed on the platform, primarily ranked based on the average value of their reported probability and magnitude. Proportion of the population exposed to the hazard is used where data is incomplete.

Google Earth Engine and external data

To provide additional context and better understand the dynamics of a hazard in a given jurisdiction, geospatial hazard data layers were developed from datasets available on Google Earth Engine and other data portals, including from specific risk indices or hazard forecasting models, as well as directly from downscaled climate projections.

Earth Engine and other external hazard data were rescaled to values ranging from 0 to 5, aligning it for easier comparison with the five-category, low-to-high questionnaire response format scale. A value of 0 represents when a hazard is not expected to impact the jurisdiction within the next 5 years. Jurisdictions without any exposure to the given hazard were omitted in this calculation, allowing the analysis to better reflect comparisons across areas actually experiencing the hazard.

Many hazard layers are available for both historical baseline conditions as well as future projections. Projected values are provided for two-decade ranges, centered on three milestone years: 2030, 2050, and 2080. Hazard projections are provided under four combined, widely used socioeconomic-climate scenarios. These scenarios represent increasingly extreme warming pathways: SSP1–RCP2.6, SSP2–4.5, SSP3–RCP7.0, and SSP5–RCP8.5. There are a few exceptions: soil erosion is only available for historical conditions, and flooding hazards are available for historical conditions, SSP2–4.5, and SSP5–RCP8.5 scenarios. Water stress includes three of the four scenarios, omitting SSP2–4.5.

For fire weather, extreme heat/heat stress, extreme cold, and extreme precipitation, downscaled climate projections from NEX-GDDP-CMIP6 were used. Hazard values were calculated for ~28-km grid cells for each year under the various scenarios. A multi-model ensemble (MME) median value was calculated for each cell using all available climate models. This provides a central, representative value across diverse climate model projections and reduces the effects of variation between models.

Hazard maps and scores for each jurisdiction were computed by spatially filtering and averaging global hazard layers within the jurisdiction's administrative boundaries. Administrative boundaries were obtained primarily through data from Overture Maps Foundation. When unavailable, boundaries were obtained from OpenStreetMap or datasets available on the UN OCHA Humanitarian Data Exchange. When no appropriate jurisdiction boundary was found, pseudo-boundaries were created using the jurisdiction's geographic center (sourced from the above or manually in Google Maps) and their reported land area.

Government actions

This tab contains three categories of information from the disclosure:

  • Adaptation goals (Question 5.1 in the questionnaire)
  • Actions (Question 9.1)
  • Projects (Question 9.3)

Adaptation goals are adaptation targets based on climate hazards and are typically qualitatively defined.

Actions are defined here as the interventions taken to achieve adaptation goals and carry out a given strategy, which can include policies, projects, programs, partnerships, and more. Adaptation action data may include information on the sectors it impacts, attributes of resilience it enhances, co-benefits of the project, population affected, expectations for enhanced resilience timeframe, and funding sources and costs.

Projects are specific climate-related initiatives for which jurisdictions are seeking funding, as reported via the questionnaire. Information about projects may include funding needs, project description, financing models, and more.

In addition, the Government Actions tab indicates whether the jurisdiction has completed a Climate Risk and Vulnerability Assessment, or has disclosed that they plan to do so.

Solutions

This tab presents information on relevant actions being taken by other peer jurisdictions, derived from actions reported in the questionnaire. Jurisdictions highlighted here are those in the same biome as a given city, state, or region, with actions that address one or more of the top 4 hazards of the given city, state, or region. Solutions highlight the types of actions that jurisdictions are taking to adapt to climate hazards. Cities, states, and regions choose from an extensive list of solutions that represents a given action when reporting the actions they are taking.

These relevant actions from other jurisdictions in the same ecoregion are surfaced to help users explore what others are doing to adapt to climate hazards. Actions from these jurisdictions are collated and then grouped by solution category (e.g. engineered and built environment actions, behavioural actions) and solution type (e.g. sea walls and coastal protection structures, genetic techniques). The full list of solution types can be found under section 9.1 in the CDP questionnaire guide. From this list, the top 25 solutions are chosen based on those with the highest number of associated actions reported. A maximum of 5 solutions are chosen from a given solution category to highlight a diversity of efforts. For each solution, a subset of actions are selected to display as examples.

These criteria establish a baseline level of data quality, allowing incomplete, inconsistent, or otherwise unreliable responses to be filtered out prior to analysis.
